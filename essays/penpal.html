10,000-Practice Kick

On "Penpal" by Dathan Auerbach

"In a quiet room if you press your ear against a pillow you can hear your heartbeat. As a kid, the muffled, rhythmic beats sounded like soft footsteps on a carpeted floor, and so as a kid almost every night – just as I was about to drift off to sleep – I would hear these footsteps and I would be ripped back to consciousness, terrified."

I never read horror for the horror. Reading simply doesn’t scare me; it has to be some form of visual + auditory medium like a movie or TV show. What I do read horror for is the ideas and concepts. I love stories about cool rules or creatures, such as The Left Right Game or stories from The SCP Foundation or H.P. Lovecraft, as I’m able to delve into the lore and grandeur of it all. I especially love when the world smartly adapts to this creature and phenomenon, giving it credentials and realism. In The Left Right Game, the main character is a news reporter that follows a seasoned veteran of what is supposed to be the horror phenomenon of the plot; the horror is simplified into a higher-stakes education trip, but it’s cool because human detection and effort is being applied to make the horror more real. The SCP Foundation stories follow the same trope– even moreso. Every story is literally scientific documentation, essentially narrowing the scope down to facts, evidence, and chains of events. It allows the reader to fill the horror in for themselves while getting the hard facts on what the Big Bad of the document is.

“See you tonight.”

Veronica didn’t show.

After reading Penpal, I realized that reading horror actually can scare me

I texted her the next day asking if she was alright and I enquired as to why she didn’t show the previous night… She simply said,

“See you again. Soon.”

If I liken my average horror reading to Bruce Lee’s 10,000 quote, most of the horror that I’ve read fall under the 10,000 Kicks category. They explore many different ideas, don’t try too hard on the technicals of horror execution, and introduce and flesh out many cool concepts and worlds that could be horrific had you experienced them yourself.

“...my mother, who had been really nice as of late, asked me if I was OK. I told her that I hadn’t heard from Veronica in days.”

On the other hand, Penpal is a 10,000 Times book. It is set in a mundane town, with a normal kid trying to live a normal childhood, but the author clearly knows how to write HORROR. There are no new ideas or concepts to describe, allowing Auerbach to focus purely on manipulating your emotional and physiological state through text. The pacing is amazing, the delivery is amazing, the organization is amazing. It made me realize that writing good horror means being good at suspense, raising and balancing the reader on a thin line and reeling them in like a bluff in Poker.

She looked confused.

“Veronica’s dead, sweetheart. Oh God, I thought you knew. She died on the last day you visited her. Oh baby, she died weeks ago.”

The story is written to convey horror. Not a horror concept.

“Then why was her phone still on?”

I exploded, “WHY DID IT TAKE THEM SO LONG TO SHUT OFF HER GODDAMNED PHONE?!”

I always cringe when hearing about overused themes and messages, so I hate having to explain/market this story to friends. Afterall, an easy (and valid) way to sum up the horror in the story is the horror of humanity and how humans themselves are already scary enough on their own, which, as a theme, just feels so worn out at this point. What I think differentiates this from the average “I’m going to Jeff-the-Kill you!” online horror-slop with a similar message is that it’s actually good writing– loaded word intended. A lot of horror stories on the net are by novice writers that are just starting their journey through posting on public forums; Auerbach is clearly a bit more seasoned. In addition to the piece’s suspense, it also has amazingly realistic dialogue that adds to the suspense and sometimes even provides a break from it allowing the reader some time to breathe. The fact that the main characters are kids tangibly adds to the plot through interactions and quips. I also really like that the story is written like a ghost story, but it’s not; the monster feels like a smoky ghost or overwhelming demon, but it’s not. It’s just some guy.

“See you again. Soon.”

What a breath of fresh air I didn't know I needed... Or, I don't know...Maybe it's still slop.
