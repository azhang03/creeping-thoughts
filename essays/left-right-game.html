I Want A Path That Thinks For Me

On "The Left Right Game" by Jack Anderson

The rules of the Left/Right game are simple. Get in your car and take a drive. Take a left, then the next possible road on the right, then the next possible left. Repeat the process ad infinitum, until you wind up somewhere... new.

One of the greats for sure, and one of the longest creepypastas I’ve ever read. It took the new (at the time) but quickly overused horror trope of Follow this curated list of rules else you invoke the wrath of some horror and built a fleshed out story surrounding it, in addition to a fresh and vibrant take on the concept. To be candid, I do think it has fundamental shortocming vibe-wise: the actual horror begins to taper off halfway through to make way for more action and drama, becoming an esoteric journey of enlightenment rather than a suspenseful log of an eldritch land. However, the many plot points it plants and unravels throughout the story along with its impact on numerous online horror story discourses during its posting cements itself as one of my favorites purely through merit, even if I still feel unsatisfied by the momentum fall-off of its ending.

Though, to be fair, if I’d been put on a long, horrific, treacherous journey, but learned at the end that it was a path to lead me toward omniscience and cosmic intelligence, I would be ecstatic…moreso for the intelligence than the godhood part; imagine how much faster I could get my homework done, how much easier it would be to pick up social skills, how effortless it would be for me to make decisions.

That's what's so cool about these stories: the structured rules that they come with, almost like a puzzle for the reader to unravel (I need to stay focused to detect rule violations). The need to make a decision has always vexed me, a chronic inability to make a decision myself. I always need someone else involved. Maybe to offload pressure, responsibility, meaning. To avoid disappointing someone else, as everyone always knows better than me, even for me. Adrenaline junkies need to jump out of planes, workaholics need to fill their timeblocks; I can achieve the same cortisol rush more significantly and efficiently through simply thinking about needing to make a decision by myself. The worst is when it comes as a result of your own choosing– a cherry on top if the result was supposed to be positive.

In high school, I had to choose between two good colleges. It's a good problem to have. It took me weeks and liters of sweat and tears to decide; I still think about what I left, whether my choice was right. More recently, I had to choose between a graduate program and full-time position. Doing good for myself comes back to haunt me. Being ambitious only invites the world to make life more difficult, to make you come to hate success for the different options it provides.

The rules are easy to understand, but Rob says they're not so easy to follow.

I want life to be as easy as the game– that sounds wrong but I don't think it is. I want to be able to walk through a path of grids, following a predetermined matrix handed to me by some being, some world, that I can offload responsibility to. Then, all the thoughts I’ll need to think will be forward and finishing. No need to ask for the right direction, no need to think about the possibilities left. Even if I don’t want to continue on, the path will always be open to me, only to me and waiting for me to proceed. Then, I won’t be burdened by the ability to complete, the greedy want to focus on a task and not be distracted or assaulted by the ugly need to make a decision that tugs on a strand, a strand that bends life.

And in such a perfect world, on that left/right road, I may begin to write, with no longer a need to jot pros and cons, I am afforded the chance to dialogue and dictate, describe and depict the simplicity and efficiency of life as I move through it and it moves through me in conjunction and parallel, I will write and write and write, oh I’ll have so much time to write and fun doing it, stress-free with no uncertainties.

I will write and write and write.

Until there is nothing left to write.

Until I don't need to know left from right.
