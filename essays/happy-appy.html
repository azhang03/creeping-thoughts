Weight Class

On "Happy Appy" by Dronian

"After the shot with the kid trapped under the concrete, the younger kid turns to the side say 'Happy Appy, why are people running and falling from the towers?' Happy Appy turns to the camera, death smile on his face, and very coldly said three words.

'That's natural, children.'"

Yep, that's a reference to 9/11.

It really is quite bad. Like a lot of the early creepypastas that made this genre so popular to begin with, it is the literary version of a low-budget slasher film, unapologetic for what it is, the events it depicts, and its writing professionalism. Happy Appy was actually started relatively late compared to other classics like Ted the Caver, but it's the intent that matters, I suppose. Yet, of course, there cannot be peace without war. For an online horror-reader, I think one of the reasons Left/Right Game and Penpal hit hard is because of how many leagues above Happy Appy and Jeff the Killer they are. In a pool of neverending hack-and-slashes written by elementary schoolers on iPads, good works are especially noticeable and illuminated. Maybe that’s unfair though. People don’t really like smurfing in video games, right?

In the show Jujutsu Kaisen, sorcerers are blessed with the ability to control cursed energy, a semi-sentient fantasy energy that is naturally born from fear and negative emotions. With it, sorcerers can enact “binding vows,” tradeoffs that allow them to gain more from one aspect of their power in exchange for losing something else. From a writing perspective, this is really cool, as it provides a plot-convenient reason for characters to expose how their technique/power works to their opponents, and thus the audience: giving away how your power works is a negative, so your powers are blessed by cursed energy to be stronger. Whenever I talk to my friends about horrible creepypasts and why they’re so popular despite being so bad, I always joke that they’re the product of binding vows.

Exchange good writing for being immortalized as foundational. Allow bad stories to exist so the good ones can hit harder.

But that’s also not the whole story, because at some point, I saw nothing wrong with the quality of these creepypastas– as a matter of fact, I thought they were cool and enjoyed reading them. The Russian Sleep Experiments' “I AM GOD,” didn’t even make me cringe. I was a kid in a young weight class (like fighting, not bigotry), and so were these stories. Time passed, new reading experiences were gained, and I upgraded. I can now see how juvenile these old stories were, but that doesn’t mean I should hate myself for liking it back then.

Context and experience matters. When I first started college, my understanding of life hadn’t really caught up with the stage I was in. The concept of internships and careers eluded me for the entirety of freshman year, and even a bit of sophomore year; I spent entire days studying and playing League of Legends. My favorite way to play was create new accounts and smurf– punching below my weight class, inefficient in development for both game-skill and as a working person. Actually, wouldn’t this put me in the correct weight class? As I went deeper and deeper into my upperclassman years I had to confront the reality that I was extraordinarily behind the average high-achieving USC student and practically destroy myself to have a modicum of feeling that I was “catching up.” Giving grace is necessary in this case. An understanding that everyone has their own path in life. That feeling behind can also mean being so far ahead of others in the same age range– what determines weight class in this case? Age? Experience? Life stage? But what if one person never reaches a life stage that another one is in? Paths can run parallel, perhaps, but what is the actual unit of measurement? And thus, give grace. Maybe Happy Appy deserves some grace then, for what it represents for the/our fandom, and for the career stepping stone it may be for its author.

It’s all about weight class, but…not literally…I think? Because you can’t really control your density and mass-makeup, and that’s what such a thing is– in a literal sense– meant to quantify, but within the context of this–in hindsight somewhat inept– analogy, I suppose experience would allow you to elevate to a higher “weight class” or level. But even then, can you “control” it? Relationships help with understanding mindfulness and respect, interaction and discourse allow for the development of societal values and an understanding of adherence. What exactly is “growing a backbone?” How mindful can you get? How lenient can you be? At what point are you complaining too much, setting too many boundaries. When are you just a contrarian? But sometimes relationships just happen right? And my current ability to sense that Happy Appy is quite bad didn’t consciously develop; stuff like this just kind of got cliche (cliche is just an overabundance of same-weight-class material, maybe). And some people never learn from the experiences they are given. Some people just stay in their metaphorical weight class forever.

And they turn out just fine, so there’s definitely a reframe to be made here. You can look at two software engineers at Meta; one will have a near-vibe-code level workflow, and another may still be stuck to the days of old, copy-pasting Stack Overflow fixes. Both are at the same job-level, and do the same quality work. Despite being people with two different configurations/builds, they punch in the same weight class. Or maybe they’re in two different weight classes, but to the company, the weight class of their results are the same?

So...Happy Appy is not so great in many attributes, but it's still in the weight class of the greats– it represents the courage and audacity of the early age of internet horror, a long-winded troll-post. You agree that defining good stories requires nuance. The same goes for people too. Something something life, something something black and white. Yet people all the time are villainized for failure to give a strictly conforming answer– “It’s nuanced” is "dangerous" as it spurs indecision. “You need to grow a backbone.”

Ok then. For those annoyed and confused about what all this was:

Tl;dr - Happy Appy bad. Period.
