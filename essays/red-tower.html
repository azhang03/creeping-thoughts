Quality-Slop

On "The Red Tower" and other works by Thomas Ligotti.

"The ruined factory stood three stories high in an otherwise featureless landscape. Although somewhat imposing on its own terms, it occupied only the most unobtrusive place within the gray emptiness of its surroundings, its presence serving as a mere accent upon a desolate horizon."

Thomas Ligotti’s The Red Tower– and Masquerade of a Dead Sword: A Tragedie, which I also got to read– are quality slop. The concept seems crazy-stupid at first: slop is often seen as a work that is bad, cliche, and overproduced, so how can it be high quality? The word itself is a juxtaposition, but I think it sums up Ligotti’s The Red Tower quite well.

“this is just qualityslop. You only like it because it’s good” ~ Anonymous, 4chan

It’s overall a plotless moodpiece utilizing its load-bearing old-English to beautify itself as an article of writing; it scores high for writing technique and skill, and the concepts are technically scary, but it does nothing to induce horror within the audience and it can get boring and confusing at times. Masquerade of a Dead Sword is a bit better as it does have a plot, but similar to Stephen King’s exposition run-on paragraphs, it can get overly descriptive and flamboyant. It yaps: ultimately, the story ends up nowhere.

If Penpal from the previous entry is the middleground between…I don’t know… horrorslop and qualityslop… I’d say The Red Tower is at the far end of qualityslop, and with Stephen King’s works and Nick Lowe’s Dagon’s Mirror– which has similar language usage but better plot– somewhere in between. Even broader, we can say that classics like Jeff the Killer and The SCP Foundation are horror for the sake of being cool concept-wise; they rely on extrapolation. Stuff like Penpal are horror for the sake of being horror– fundamentals. Now, for The Red Tower, we’re looking at horror for the sake of being art; it’s a celebration and mastery of the actual medium of “writing.” As a matter of fact, it’s pretty similar to H.P. Lovecraft’s works. I was quite disappointed when I first read Lovecraft’s stuff to find that they’re actually fancy scary-concept horror and not scary-story horror, like OK the giant Eldritch octopus gets beaten by… a boat?

It’s like why you might prefer to have a painting of random circle and dots rather than the Mona Lisa in your minimalist living room, or prefer a casio to a Rolex; the context and what you want out of a product is what determines its value to you, and how “good” it actually is, no matter if it is high in technical quality. It’s the same vibes that make having celebrity crushes in a relationship acceptable and understandable; no one has a Micheline-starred restaurant as a daily-driver…I think.

Though that’s not to say that significant others should be likened to establishments or material objects, nor is it a commentary on acceptable/unacceptable boundaries in a relationship (such a characterization of boundaries does not/should not exist purely based on principles over self-ownership), but now that I think about it, maybe there is something to be said about “pretty-privilege” and how maybe it's simply another archetype with its own strengths and weaknesses– like a character class– rather than a higher tier of human experience, as we all know of the “class beauty that didn’t get asked out to prom because she’s too pretty” cliche, but probably not because in a brief doomscroll session during recruiting season I remember reading articles on the hiring process and how employers were more likely to send good-looking candidates to later interview rounds and give them offer letters, but of course all generalizations are dangerous as they fail to account for the true nuance of human experience, yet not generalizing is also dangerous as then inaction begets inaction and thus no change ultimately happens…

So Thomas Ligotti’s works are art– not the kind that you’d want to hang in your living room, more like something that belongs in a museum that’s meant to be appreciated for the mastery-of-medium it represents. If you’re a literature buff, you’ll like it. If you’re a theater kid, you’ll like it. If you like and prefer the “good” (as accepted by online discourse) creepypastas that are out there, this might be too much for you...It was too much for me.

Of course, the argument could also be made that “good” writing is writing that does well what it advertises itself as (ex. horror), but then that’d be saying Ligotti’s works are complete garbage…which also isn’t right.
